Codes, Permits & Inspections

Why permits exist, how inspections work, and what New Jersey actually requires for bathroom and shower projects.

On This Page

NJ Codes, Permits & Inspections

Step 1 of the Construction Order — New Jersey Bathroom & Shower Projects

This page explains how bathroom and shower construction is regulated in New Jersey, what permits and inspections actually do, and why skipping them creates risk—not savings.

This guide follows the TCNA Handbook methods for tile and wet-area assemblies, coordinated with New Jersey code requirements. Permits ensure your build meets both standards.

This is the starting point for any compliant build.

Official Standards & References

Before diving in, here are the authoritative bodies and standards that govern tile and bathroom construction in New Jersey:

Organization What They Do Official Link
TCNA (Tile Council of North America) Publishes the Handbook for Ceramic, Glass, and Stone Tile Installation—the industry bible tcnatile.com
ANSI (American National Standards Institute) Develops A108/A118/A136 standards for tile installation materials and methods ansi.org
NTCA (National Tile Contractors Association) Trade association providing training, certification, and best practices tile-assn.com
ICC (International Code Council) Publishes the IRC, IPC, and other model codes adopted by NJ iccsafe.org
NJ DCA (Department of Community Affairs) Administers the Uniform Construction Code statewide nj.gov/dca/codes

For homeowners: You don’t need to memorize these standards. But knowing they exist—and that your contractor should follow them—protects your investment.

The Governing System: New Jersey Uniform Construction Code (UCC)

All construction in New Jersey is regulated under the Uniform Construction Code (UCC) (N.J.A.C. 5:23).

Key characteristics:

  • Statewide code framework administered by the NJ Department of Community Affairs
  • Local enforcement through municipal Construction Offices
  • Model codes adopted by reference (with NJ-specific amendments)
  • Inspector discretion allowed within defined limits

The UCC adopts national model codes, including:

  • International Residential Code (IRC) — residential construction
  • International Plumbing Code (IPC) — plumbing systems
  • International Mechanical Code (IMC) — ventilation, HVAC
  • National Electrical Code (NEC) — wiring, controls, GFCI protection

Inspectors enforce the intent of the code: safety, sanitation, drainage, and durability.

Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)

The Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) is the local Construction Office or Construction Official assigned to your municipality.

Finding Your Local Construction Office

Every NJ municipality has a Construction Office. To find yours:

  1. Search “[your town] NJ construction office” online
  2. Visit NJ League of Municipalities Directory
  3. Call your town hall and ask for the Construction Department

What the AHJ Does

  • Reviews permit applications
  • Conducts inspections at required stages
  • Interprets the UCC locally
  • Approves or rejects alternative methods
  • Issues Certificates of Approval upon completion

Important Realities for Homeowners

  • Two towns may interpret details slightly differently
  • Inspectors do not approve brand names—they approve performance
  • Early communication avoids late rework
  • Building good relationships with your local office makes projects smoother

Pro tip: If your contractor dismisses permits or avoids inspections, that’s a red flag. Licensed NJ contractors (like us—HIC #13VH10808800) work with inspectors, not around them.

When a Permit Is Required

A permit is typically required when a bathroom or shower project includes:

  • New or relocated plumbing (drain, supply lines, venting)
  • Drain or vent modifications of any kind
  • Shower or tub replacement (even “like for like” in many towns)
  • Waterproofing system replacement
  • Structural or framing changes (walls, benches, niches)
  • Electrical work (lighting, outlets, ventilation fans, heated floors)

Work That May Be Exempt

Purely cosmetic work—such as painting, replacing faucet handles, or swapping a toilet of the same type—may be exempt. However:

  • Waterproofing work almost always triggers permitting
  • Drain work almost always triggers permitting
  • “Cosmetic” is narrowly defined—when in doubt, call your town

Permits protect both the homeowner and the contractor. They create accountability and legal documentation.

How to Apply for a Permit in New Jersey

Step-by-Step Process for Homeowners

1. Determine Permit Requirements

  • Call or visit your local Construction Office
  • Describe the scope of work in detail
  • Ask specifically about plumbing, electrical, and building sub-permits

2. Gather Required Documents Typical requirements include:

  • Completed application form (available at your town hall or online)
  • Property survey or plot plan
  • Scope of work description
  • Contractor’s NJ HIC license number and insurance certificates
  • Plans or drawings (may be required for larger projects)

3. Submit the Application

  • In person at the Construction Office, or
  • Online through your municipality’s portal (many NJ towns now offer this)
  • Pay the permit fee (varies by municipality and scope)

4. Wait for Approval

  • Simple permits may be issued same-day
  • Complex projects may require plan review (1-2 weeks typical)
  • You’ll receive a permit card to post at the job site

5. Schedule Inspections

  • Your contractor schedules inspections at required stages
  • Inspections must pass before work proceeds
  • Final inspection triggers the Certificate of Approval

Permit Fees in New Jersey

Permit fees vary by municipality but typically include:

Fee Type Typical Range
Building permit (base) $75-$150
Plumbing sub-permit $50-$100
Electrical sub-permit $50-$100
Plan review (if required) $50-$200

Note: Some towns calculate fees based on project value. Always confirm fees before starting.

What Permits Actually Do (Plain Language)

A permit:

  • Establishes a legal scope of work on file with your municipality
  • Requires inspections at critical stages (checkpoints)
  • Creates a permanent record of compliant construction
  • Protects your property value and insurability

A permit does not:

  • Guarantee quality finishes or aesthetics
  • Dictate tile brand, style, or layout
  • Replace contractor skill or professionalism

It ensures the work meets minimum life-safety and sanitation standards.

The Inspection Process: What to Expect

Typical Inspection Sequence for Bathroom/Shower Projects

While details vary by municipality, most bathroom and shower builds follow this inspection order:

Stage What’s Inspected Typical Duration
1. Rough Plumbing Drain location, slope intent, venting, supply lines 15-30 minutes
2. Rough Framing Structural changes, blocking, benches, niches 15-30 minutes
3. Rough Electrical Wiring, boxes, GFCI protection, fan venting 15-30 minutes
4. Waterproofing/Pan Slope, drain integration, water containment 15-45 minutes
5. Final Fixtures installed, systems operational, safety verified 30-60 minutes

How to Schedule Inspections

  1. Your contractor typically handles this (make sure this is in your contract)
  2. Call the Construction Office or use their online portal
  3. Provide the permit number and requested inspection type
  4. Be available—inspections are usually scheduled for AM or PM windows
  5. Ensure the work area is accessible and well-lit

What Happens During an Inspection

The inspector will:

  • Verify work matches the permitted scope
  • Check for code compliance at visible assemblies
  • Note any deficiencies that must be corrected
  • Issue a pass, conditional pass, or fail

If an inspection fails:

  • The inspector will explain what needs correction
  • Your contractor makes the repairs
  • A re-inspection is scheduled
  • No additional fee for first re-inspection (typically)

Homeowner tip: You have the right to be present during inspections. It’s a good opportunity to learn about your project and ask questions.

Working With Your Municipality: Tips for Homeowners

Before the Project

  • Call early — Don’t wait until demo day to ask about permits
  • Be specific — Describe exactly what you’re changing (shower size, drain location, electrical additions)
  • Get it in writing — Ask for permit requirements via email if possible
  • Verify contractor licensing — Ask for their NJ HIC number and check it at NJ Consumer Affairs

During the Project

  • Post the permit — Keep the permit card visible at the job site
  • Don’t cover work early — Waterproofing must be inspectable before tile
  • Communicate delays — If schedules change, let inspectors know
  • Keep records — Photos of rough plumbing, waterproofing, and flood tests

After the Project

  • Get your Certificate of Approval — This document proves compliant completion
  • Keep it with your home records — Important for resale and insurance
  • Report issues — If you discover code violations later, contact your town

Why Inspectors Focus on What They Do

Inspectors focus on items that:

  • Cannot be corrected after tile is installed
  • Affect sanitation and moisture control
  • Create long-term liability if missed

They are not evaluating aesthetics. They are verifying assemblies—the systems that keep water where it belongs and protect your home’s structure.

TCNA Compliance: Why It Matters

The TCNA Handbook for Ceramic, Glass, and Stone Tile Installation is the definitive reference for tile installation methods in North America.

What TCNA Provides

  • Numbered installation methods (e.g., B421, B422, B412) specifying assembly layers
  • Material requirements for substrates, membranes, and setting materials
  • Performance criteria for deflection, flatness, and moisture management

Key TCNA Methods for Bathroom/Shower Projects

Method Description Application
B412 Cementitious backer unit with waterproof membrane Shower walls over wood or steel studs
B414 Foam backer board with bonded waterproofing Lightweight shower wall systems
B421 Bonded waterproof membrane shower receptor Modern shower floors with surface-applied waterproofing
B422 Shower receptor with traditional membrane Clamping drain systems
EJ171 Movement joint design guide Where soft joints are required

Why Homeowners Should Care

When your contractor says “we follow TCNA methods,” they’re referencing a specific, documented assembly—not an opinion. This creates:

  • Accountability — Methods can be verified
  • Consistency — Same approach across projects
  • Defensibility — If problems arise, there’s a standard to reference

Example: TCNA Method B421 specifies a bonded waterproof membrane shower receptor with surface-applied waterproofing—including required slope, drain type, and membrane continuity.

ANSI Standards: The Technical Foundation

ANSI (American National Standards Institute) publishes the technical standards that underpin TCNA methods:

Standard What It Covers Why It Matters
ANSI A108 Installation methods for ceramic tile Defines how tile is properly installed
ANSI A118.1 Dry-set portland cement mortar Standard thinset specifications
ANSI A118.4 Modified dry-set cement mortar Enhanced mortars for wet areas
ANSI A118.10 Load-bearing bonded waterproof membranes Critical for shower pans
ANSI A118.12 Crack isolation membranes Prevents substrate cracks from telegraphing
ANSI A136.1 Organic adhesives (mastics) When mastics can/cannot be used

What ANSI A118.10 Means for Your Shower

ANSI A118.10 is the standard for load-bearing, bonded waterproof membranes—the type used in modern surface-applied shower waterproofing. A membrane that meets this standard:

  • Can support tile loads without delamination
  • Maintains waterproofing under normal use
  • Has been tested for adhesion, flexibility, and water resistance

Homeowner insight: Ask your contractor if their waterproofing system is ANSI A118.10 compliant. If they don’t know what that means, consider it a warning sign.

Where to Access ANSI Standards

  • Purchase directly from ANSI Webstore
  • Reference through TCNA Handbook (includes relevant excerpts)
  • Trade associations like NTCA provide member access

Approved Alternatives & Performance-Based Approval

The UCC allows inspectors to approve alternative methods when:

  • The intent of the code is satisfied
  • Performance is equal or superior to prescriptive requirements
  • The system is coherent and documented

This is why mixing incompatible systems often fails inspection—performance cannot be verified when components from different systems are combined.

Common Misunderstandings

Myth Reality
“It passed inspection before, so it must be fine” Past approvals do not override current code requirements
“Tile standards are code” Tile standards guide best practice; inspectors enforce building/plumbing code
“Curbless means no rules” Curbless showers are performance-based, not exempt from requirements
“We don’t need a permit for this” When in doubt, call your town—unpermitted work creates liability
“Inspectors are just trying to slow us down” Inspectors are protecting you and your investment
“TCNA is just a suggestion” TCNA methods represent industry consensus on proper installation

What Homeowners Should Ask Up Front

Before your project begins, ask your contractor:

  • Is a permit required for this scope?
  • Will the shower pan or waterproofing be inspected?
  • Are flood tests expected or recommended?
  • Are there local interpretations affecting curbless showers?
  • Which TCNA methods will be used?
  • Is the waterproofing system ANSI A118.10 compliant?
  • Who schedules and attends inspections?

Clear answers early prevent delays and redesigns later.

Key Takeaways

  • New Jersey uses a statewide code with local enforcement
  • Permits establish scope and inspection checkpoints
  • Inspectors verify function and safety, not appearance
  • TCNA and ANSI standards define professional tile installation
  • Early coordination with your municipality reduces cost and risk
  • Licensed contractors work with inspectors, not around them
